About the Team

We are a small interdisciplinary team of two Computer Science students and one Mechatronics engineer. Our skills span full-stack web development, embedded systems, and data-driven modeling. What unites us is a shared curiosity about space and a belief that scientific data should be accessible, engaging, and understandable to everyone.

Why This Project?

Asteroids may seem like distant threats, but the discovery of near-Earth objects such as “Impactor-2025” highlights the very real possibility of an impact. Most existing tools are either too technical for public audiences or too simplistic for meaningful analysis. We chose this challenge because it blends our interests in space, simulation, and visualization, and allows us to contribute something impactful: a tool that makes planetary defense data approachable for scientists, policymakers, and the public alike.

Our Data Sources

On the backend, we built a FastAPI service that wraps NASA’s NEO and SBDB APIs into a single clean endpoint. Given an asteroid ID, it returns just the orbital elements required for trajectory reconstruction (a, e, i, Ω, ω, M), plus basic physical properties (diameter, hazard flag) and closest Earth approach (date, miss distance, relative velocity). This lets the frontend simulation work directly with clean inputs without worrying about API inconsistencies or bulky JSON.

Our Vision

We aim to create an interactive tool where users can explore “what-if” scenarios—adjusting asteroid parameters, testing deflection strategies, and seeing the predicted outcomes. Whether as an educational tool or a decision-support platform, our goal is to bridge the gap between complex orbital science and accessible, intuitive visualizations that empower better understanding of planetary risks.
